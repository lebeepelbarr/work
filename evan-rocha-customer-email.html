How Evan turned a fire-damaged multifamily into a $210K fee

This week's deal comes from Evan Rocha out in Fresno, and it's one of the best saves featured in this series so far.

Evan locked up a fire-damaged 16-bed multifamily from a seller who lived out of town and was only in Fresno for the day. His team responded within 60 seconds of getting the lead, met him within the hour, and had the property under contract. Twenty-one days later, the deal closed with a $210,000 assignment fee.

It almost didn't.

The strategy behind the deal

• Why speed mattered: the seller was only in town for the day. If Evan's team had gotten back to him on Monday, there is no deal. They responded in 60 seconds, and that's the whole reason the contract has Evan's name on it.
• Why he wholesaled it: with roughly $400,000 of fire damage, a flip means months of construction and insurance headaches with your capital stuck the whole time. Evan took the faster, cleaner exit and put his money right back to work.
• Why the numbers worked: the ARV was around $1.4M with about $400K of work. Buying at $500K left enough room for Evan's $210,000 fee while the end buyer still made good money. When the numbers work for everyone, deals survive a lot of stress.

What you can learn from this

“Be transparent with your buyers. If the numbers work for them, then they shouldn’t have a problem with your fee.”

— Evan Rocha

Here's the part worth studying closely. The fee isn't what almost killed this deal. A surprise mortgage payoff was. Then, right when everything looked back on track, the buyer's lender refused to fund the assignment.

How Evan worked through both setbacks, and why complete transparency ended up saving the deal, is the biggest lesson in this week's breakdown.

What Evan had running in REsimpli

The story isn't just the $210,000. It's that Evan had systems keeping the deal alive when everything started going wrong:

• Speed to Lead got his team in front of the seller while he was still in town.
• Every contract, photo, offer, and note lived on one deal record, so nobody was digging through emails when things got complicated.
• When the payoff problem hit escrow, all the documentation was already right there, which made untangling it a lot easier.
• The pipeline kept everyone on the same page from contract through assignment and closing.
